六大茶类

liù dà chá lèi

Chinese tea is a single leaf — Camellia sinensis — transformed six different ways by oxidation and processing. This path walks the full spectrum, from fresh-green to aged-dark, then turns to ceremony and craft.

The Six Classes

六大茶类 · liù dà chá lèi

China's tea taxonomy is built on one variable: how much oxygen the leaf is allowed to touch before heat stops the process. At one pole sits green tea (no oxidation); at the other, fully-oxidised black tea. In between come white, yellow, oolong, and dark teas. Post-fermented dark teas — including 普洱 Pǔ'ěr — form a class of their own, aged rather than simply dried.

Herbal tisanes (花草茶 huācǎo chá) fall outside the six-class system entirely: they use no tea leaf, so they carry no caffeine and no place on the oxidation ladder.

绿茶 · Green

绿茶 · Green Tea — Unoxidised. The oldest processed form; steamed in Japan, pan-fired in China. Longjing and Biluochun sit at the top of the canon.

白茶 · White

白茶 · White Tea — Barely touched: the bud and young leaves are simply withered and dried. Silvery, subtle, and fast-rising in international markets.

黄茶 · Yellow

黄茶 · Yellow Tea — The rarest class. A brief smothering step (渥黄 wò huáng) after firing mutes the grassiness of green without full oxidation.

乌龙 · Oolong

乌龙茶 · Oolong Tea — Partially oxidised (15–85%), shaped into balls or twisted strips. The broadest flavour range of any class: floral Tieguanyin to roasted rock oolongs.

红茶 · Black

红茶 · Red Tea (Black Tea) — Fully oxidised. What the West calls "black tea," Chinese calls red — for the colour of the liquor, not the leaf. Keemun and Dianhong are the benchmarks.

黑茶 · Dark

黑茶 · Dark Tea & Pu-erh — Post-fermented and often aged for years or decades. 普洱 Pǔ'ěr from Yunnan is the most famous; compressed cakes can be stored like wine.

花草茶 · Tisanes

花草茶 · Tisanes & Herbal Infusions — Outside the six: chrysanthemum, osmanthus, rose, and dozens of medicinal herbs steeped without a leaf of Camellia sinensis.

工夫茶 · Ceremony

工夫茶 · Gongfu Tea — The slow art of brewing. Small pot, tiny cups, multiple short steeps — a discipline that turns preparation into attention.

茶具 · Utensils

茶具 · Tea Implements — Gaiwans, Yixing clay pots, fairness cups, and bamboo tongs: the material culture of the table.

茶文化 · Tea Culture — Tea and politics, tea and poetry, tea and social hierarchy: how the leaf became a thread woven through all of Chinese civilisation.
