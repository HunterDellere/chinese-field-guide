      <h1 class="topic-hero-title">朝代</h1>
      <span class="topic-hero-title-py">cháodài</span>
      <p class="topic-hero-desc">Four thousand years, one recurring logic: a house rises on military conquest, consolidates through law or culture, reaches a peak of art and administration, then fractures under the weight of its own contradictions. This path walks the full ladder, from the legendary Three Dynasties to the revolutionary ruptures of the twentieth century.</p>
    </header>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="overview"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><h2>The Dynastic Cycle</h2></div>
    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="史">
      <div class="scholar-label">天命 · tiān mìng — the Mandate of Heaven</div>
      <p>Chinese historians developed the concept of 天命 tiān mìng — the Mandate of Heaven — to explain why dynasties rise and fall. A ruler who governs well holds heaven's approval; one who grows corrupt, cruel, or simply unlucky loses it. Floods, famines, and rebellions are read not as random catastrophe but as heaven withdrawing its sanction. The next dynasty inherits the mandate by defeating the last, and the cycle restarts.</p>
      <p>The framework is both a political theory and a historiographical one: every official dynastic history (正史 zhèngshǐ) was written by the succeeding dynasty, which had every incentive to document its predecessor's moral failures. Reading the dynasties in sequence is therefore also reading the self-image of Chinese civilisation — its preferred explanation of why things change.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="path"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><h2>Reading Path</h2></div>

    <div class="cards">
      <div class="card c-teal">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">字 · The Character</span>
        </div>
        <p><a href="../../pages/characters/guo2_国.html">国 · guó</a> — Start here: the glyph for "nation" or "state," an enclosure radical surrounding a jade symbol for kingship — a compact image of territory, authority, and legitimacy that every dynasty claimed to embody.</p>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-teal">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">中国 · The Name</span>
        </div>
        <p><a href="../../pages/characters/zhong1_中.html">中 · zhōng</a> — The "middle" in 中国: not geographical centre but cultural centrality, the self-designation of a civilisation that considered itself the axis of the known world.</p>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-ochre">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">夏商周 · The Three Houses</span>
        </div>
        <p><a href="../../pages/history/topic_xia_shang_zhou.html">夏商周 · Xia, Shang & Zhou</a> — The founding triad: the semi-mythic Xia, the Shang who left us oracle-bone script, and the Zhou who gave China Confucius, Laozi, and the Hundred Schools of Thought.</p>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-ochre">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">秦朝 · The First Emperor</span>
        </div>
        <p><a href="../../pages/history/topic_qin_shihuang.html">秦始皇 · Qin Shihuang</a> — The man who ended four centuries of Warring States in fifteen years: standardised weights, measures, and script; built the first Great Wall; and then burned the books. The Qin lasted fifteen years and shaped everything.</p>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-ochre">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">汉朝 · The Han</span>
        </div>
        <p><a href="../../pages/history/topic_hanchao.html">汉朝 · The Han Dynasty</a> — Four centuries of Confucian consolidation, Silk Road expansion, and the invention of the bureaucratic examination system that would govern China for two thousand years. The ethnic majority still calls itself 汉 hàn.</p>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-sienna">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">唐朝 · The Golden Age</span>
        </div>
        <p><a href="../../pages/history/topic_tangchao.html">唐朝 · The Tang Dynasty</a> — The cosmopolitan peak: Chang'an was the largest city on earth, Tang poetry the highest register of the literary tradition, and the empire stretched from Korea to Central Asia. Buddhism flowered; so did gunpowder.</p>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-sienna">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">宋朝 · The Age of Refinement</span>
        </div>
        <p><a href="../../pages/history/topic_songchao.html">宋朝 · The Song Dynasty</a> — Militarily weak, culturally brilliant: the Song invented paper money, movable type, and the magnetic compass, and produced China's greatest landscape painters and Neo-Confucian philosophers.</p>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-sienna">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">元朝 · The Mongol Century</span>
        </div>
        <p><a href="../../pages/history/topic_yuanchao.html">元朝 · The Yuan Dynasty</a> — Kublai Khan's China: the only time the whole empire was ruled by outsiders, connecting the Middle Kingdom to a world-spanning Mongol trade network that Marco Polo rode into myth.</p>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-violet">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">明朝 · The Great Wall</span>
        </div>
        <p><a href="../../pages/history/topic_mingchao.html">明朝 · The Ming Dynasty</a> — Builders of the Wall as it stands today, dispatchers of Zheng He's treasure fleets, and authors of the great vernacular novels; also the dynasty that retreated inward, closed the ports, and set the stage for its own fall.</p>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-violet">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">清朝 · The Last Dynasty</span>
        </div>
        <p><a href="../../pages/history/topic_qingchao.html">清朝 · The Qing Dynasty</a> — Manchu rulers who doubled the empire's territory, presided over a long cultural flourishing, then spent their final century absorbing treaty-port humiliations until revolution ended three thousand years of imperial rule.</p>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-red">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">辛亥 · The Revolution</span>
        </div>
        <p><a href="../../pages/history/topic_xinhai.html">辛亥革命 · The 1911 Revolution</a> — The year the last emperor abdicated: Sun Yat-sen's republicans dismantled the dynastic system, ending the cycle that had run since the Xia, and opened the contested, violent question of what China would become.</p>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-red">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">文革 · The Break</span>
        </div>
        <p><a href="../../pages/history/topic_wenge.html">文化大革命 · The Cultural Revolution</a> — Mao's attempt to destroy the past entirely: temples razed, scholars sent to fields, classical texts denounced. Understanding the Cultural Revolution is understanding why so much traditional culture needed to be rebuilt from memory.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
